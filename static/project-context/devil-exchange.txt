==============================
CASE STUDY: DUKE DINING UX RESEARCH
==============================

Author: CJ Frederickson (team-based project)
Project Type: UX Research + Product Strategy
Focus: Improving Duke University's dining system through user research and product design

==============================
PROJECT OVERVIEW
==============================

This project analyzes the user experience of Duke University's dining system and proposes a solution to improve flexibility and reduce financial inefficiencies.

The core problem:
- Students are locked into fixed dining plans
- This leads to either:
  - Excess unused food points (waste)
  - Running out of food points early (financial stress)

The proposed solution:
- A peer-to-peer food point exchange system
- Students can buy and sell food points at a discounted rate

Goal:
- Reduce waste
- Improve financial fairness
- Increase flexibility in dining usage

==============================
CURRENT SYSTEM (FACTS)
==============================

- Students purchase meal plans with "food points"
- 1 food point = 1 USD
- Points are used across campus dining locations
- Plans are fixed at the beginning of the semester

Key limitations:
1. Fixed allocation → cannot adjust mid-semester
2. No refunds for unused points
3. Points expire ("use it or lose it")
4. Students often:
   - overspend at the end
   - or run out early
5. No system exists for transferring points between students

==============================
CORE USER PROBLEM
==============================

Students cannot match their dining plan to real behavior.

This creates two major inefficiencies:
- Financial waste (unused points)
- Financial stress (running out early)

88.3% of students experience imbalance (too many or too few points)

==============================
USER PERSONAS
==============================

1. UNDER-ESTIMATOR
- Runs out of food points early
- Cannot afford out-of-pocket meals
- Needs cheaper access to additional food points

2. OVER-ESTIMATOR
- Has excess unused food points
- Wants to recover lost value
- Feels frustrated wasting money

3. BALANCED USER
- Usually manages points well
- Occasionally needs flexibility
- Wants backup options to avoid risk

==============================
UX RESEARCH METHODS
==============================

1. USER INTERVIEWS
- Explored:
  - spending behavior
  - point tracking habits
  - willingness to buy/sell points
- Key finding:
  - Strong demand for flexibility
  - High willingness to use exchange system

2. "I LIKE, I WISH, WHAT IF"
- "I Like": convenience, variety, tracking
- "I Wish": rollover points, flexibility, transparency
- "What If": transferable/liquid points, earning value back

3. LOVE/BREAKUP LETTER
- Emotional responses to system
- Revealed frustration with rigidity and waste

4. EMPATHY MAPS
- Captured what users:
  - say, think, feel, do
- Example:
  - Running out → stress, skipping meals
  - Excess → frustration, forced spending

==============================
KEY INSIGHTS
==============================

1. FLEXIBILITY IS THE CORE NEED
- Students want adjustable systems
- Fixed plans do not reflect real usage

2. FINANCIAL INEFFICIENCY IS MAJOR
- 50% feel money is wasted
- Taxes make refilling points expensive

3. WASTE IS A SYSTEMIC ISSUE
- Points expire → forced spending
- No recovery of unused value

4. DEMAND FOR EXCHANGE SYSTEM
- 100% of participants willing to buy/sell points

5. SECURITY IS CRITICAL
- Users want:
  - secure transactions
  - identity verification
  - trust in system

6. UNIVERSITY INVOLVEMENT (MIXED)
- Some want oversight for legitimacy
- Others fear added restrictions/fees

==============================
PROPOSED SOLUTION
==============================

A peer-to-peer food point exchange platform where:

- Students with excess points can SELL
- Students with shortages can BUY at discounted rates

Benefits:

For buyers:
- cheaper than university pricing
- avoids running out of food

For sellers:
- recovers value from unused points
- reduces waste

System goals:
- fair pricing
- secure transactions
- flexible usage
- reduced financial inefficiency

==============================
DESIGN CONSIDERATIONS
==============================

The system must include:

- Secure payments
- Identity verification
- Transaction tracking
- Optional filters (price, seller, etc.)
- Balanced university oversight

Potential tradeoffs:
- Too much regulation → reduces value
- Too little → creates trust issues

==============================
FINAL TAKEAWAY
==============================

The current dining system creates avoidable financial inefficiencies due to rigidity.

A peer-to-peer exchange system introduces:
- flexibility
- fairness
- improved resource distribution

If implemented correctly, it:
- reduces waste
- lowers student financial stress
- aligns the system with real user behavior

==============================

1. USER INTERVIEWS
- Conducted semi-structured interviews with Duke students
- Explored:
  - dining habits
  - financial decision-making
  - food point usage patterns
  - willingness to buy/sell points
- Key takeaway:
  - Strong demand for flexibility and willingness to participate in an exchange system

CJ's role:
- Helped design interview questions
- Conducted interviews
- Synthesized responses into actionable insights

2. ETHNOGRAPHIC ANALYSIS
- Focused on real student behavior rather than theoretical assumptions
- Observed how students:
  - track food points
  - adjust spending habits
  - respond to running out or having excess
- Captured behavioral patterns such as:
  - rationing food
  - overspending at end of semester
  - borrowing or informal exchanges

Key insight:
Students adapt their behavior around system constraints, often in inefficient or stressful ways.

3. USER PERSONAS (CREATED BY CJ AND TEAM)

CJ helped create structured personas based on observed behavioral patterns:

- Under-estimator → runs out of points early

Purpose of personas:
